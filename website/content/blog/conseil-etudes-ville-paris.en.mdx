The national debate on consulting firms in the public sphere has been dominated, since the 2022 French Senate report, by a few iconic names: McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Roland Berger, plus the audit Big Four (EY, KPMG, Deloitte, Mazars). At the scale of a large city such as Paris, the actual composition of the consulting-and-study portfolio can be read directly from public data. That is the subject of this article.

This investigation describes, based on public contracts notified by the City that year, who actually receives Parisian public money under the "consulting and studies" label, for how much, and on what topics. It does not cover delegated public service contracts, contracts of CASVP or social-housing operators, or contracts of attached public operators. It covers only City contracts in the strict sense, within a perimeter identified as "consulting, project-management assistance, audit, study, expertise".

## Method: a filter by subject and CPV family

The **151 public contracts** retained correspond to the intersection of two filters:
- either the contract's **subject** contains a consulting keyword ("conseil", "étude", "audit", "AMO", "assistance", "expertise", "évaluation", "schéma directeur")
- or the **CPV family** (European public-procurement classification) is "Business services"

The perimeter therefore covers **strictly advisory contracts** (strategic consulting, one-off audits) and **AMO contracts** — assistance à maîtrise d'ouvrage, project-management assistance — which form a dominant share of real municipal consulting. It **does not include**: pure works contracts, supplies, delegated public service contracts, operating contracts.

Cumulative total on this perimeter: **€87.6 million** (maximum notified amounts). That is around **0.9% of the City's operating budget** in 2024 (~€9.4Bn) — an order of magnitude in line with large French municipalities.

## Why the composition differs from the national scale

Three complementary explanations help understand the gap between the national image and the Parisian reality:

### 1. The City has a large, structured administration
Paris employs around 50,000 municipal and départemental staff (a figure regularly cited in City activity reports and social reports). That size makes it possible to **internalise** most strategic functions that central administrations — often smaller in specialised headcount — are forced to outsource.

### 2. Parisian needs are operational, not strategic
City orders for consulting and studies are massively oriented towards **technical AMO** (district heating, Finance IS, hydraulics, signage, audio-video) — areas where generalist Big Four firms have historically had no specific competitive advantage. The active players (MC2I, AEC, Manergy, Sémaphores) are French firms specialised in their niche, often small or mid-sized, sometimes based in Paris for decades.

### 3. City contracts are frequently lotted and technical
Many contracts are structured as **narrow technical lots** ("legal-financial AMO district heating, lot 1") that discourage generalist strategy firms. The latter typically position themselves on broader, more visible missions — which are more commonly found in central administrations (ministries, national operators).

## Important methodological caveats

The analysis presented covers contracts where the contractor is identifiable by name. Two pockets of data raise issues for a fully exhaustive reading:

### Multi-awardee contracts (€24M in 2024)
**2 contracts** out of 151 are notified under the label "MULTI-AWARDEE CONTRACT" for a cumulative amount of €24M (27% of the total). These are **framework agreements** with several contractors selected simultaneously, whose actual list does not appear in the Open Data Paris export. To reconstruct the real contractors, one would have to cross-reference individual purchase orders — which are not, to our knowledge, systematically published.

### Contracts with no supplier name (57 contracts, €18.6M)
**57 contracts** out of 151 (38% by count, 21% by amount) have an empty or missing "supplier_name" field in the export. This may reflect a technical defect (missing data at export time, unhandled exception) or a completeness issue on the portal. Either way, those €18.6M cannot be attributed to identified firms.

**In total**, around **48% of the cumulative amount** of 2024 consulting-studies contracts is attributable to identified firms. The remaining 52% is partly "invisible" in the sense that it cannot be attributed by name to a specific actor. This partial opacity probably does not change the overall picture (nothing suggests the Big Four would be massively hidden behind multi-awardee contracts), but it limits the precision of the table.

<aside className="fx-sidebar">

### Why 38% of contracts have no name in the export

This is one of the structural weaknesses of the Open Data Paris portal for public contracts. The CSV exports are generated from an administrative management database that does not treat the "contractor" field as mandatory at the time of notification. For very specific contracts (urgency, negotiated contracts without prior publication, contracts with deferred publication), the contractor may be entered later or via another channel.

The **DECP** (Données Essentielles de la Commande Publique) published on data.gouv.fr cover part of this gap — they require the contractor to be published within 15 days of notification. Systematically cross-referencing the City portal with DECP data.gouv would fill the majority of these 57 nameless contracts. This reconciliation work is, at this stage, not automated in the open-public-data pipeline.

</aside>

## Comparison with central administrations

For framing: according to the **March 2022 French Senate report on consulting firms**, certain central administrations (ministries, national agencies) display "consulting-studies / operating budget" ratios that can reach **2 to 3%**. Paris at ~0.9% is well below that.

The national debate on the weight of consulting firms in public decision-making is therefore **not playing out at the scale of large French cities**. It is playing out at the scale of central administrations, where:
- Outsourced missions are more strategic (structural reforms, multi-year plans)
- Strategy firms (McKinsey, Roland Berger…) have historically had their core target market
- Financial ratios are higher

At the municipal scale, and in Paris in particular, **the players and the topics are different**.

## What these figures do not say

This analysis stops at what the 2024 public contracts published by the City make it possible to measure. It does not say:

- **Who pays what at the level of attached operators** — the contracts of the public social-housing operators (Paris Habitat, RIVP, Elogie-Siemp), CASVP, Paris Musées are not in this perimeter. Some of the consulting consumed by the "extended City sphere" sits there, in volumes likely greater than those of City contracts in the strict sense.
- **Evolution over time** — at this date, the open-public-data pipeline has not enriched CPV codes for contracts before 2024, which prevents a robust multi-year trajectory analysis on this perimeter. That is upcoming work.
- **The quality of the consulting delivered** — a firm can be very well rated (solid deliverables, useful recommendations) for a modest amount, or disappointing for a high amount. Notification resolutions only capture the *how much*, not the *how well*.

What it does say, in one sentence: **the profile of firms receiving Parisian public money in 2024 — French firms specialised in technical AMO at the top, international IT-services providers next — differs from the composition emphasised in the national debate on McKinsey and the Big Four**. This difference does not settle the question of whether the City "outsources well" or "outsources poorly" — that is a separate evaluation question. It indicates that the nature of the contracted providers is primarily technical and operational, rather than generalist strategy consulting.
