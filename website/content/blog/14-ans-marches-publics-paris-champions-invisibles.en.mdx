Public debate on Paris public-procurement suppliers tends to focus on two categories: consultancies and the major construction groups that worked on Olympic projects. The actual structure of the City contract portfolio over 14 years is different, and the concentration there is more pronounced.

This analysis ranks, across the 14 most recent years published by the Open Data Paris portal (2013-2024 + partial 2025 data), **the 15 suppliers with the highest cumulative City contracts**. The ranking is dominated by **waste collection**, **construction-roadworks**, **urban furniture** and **industrial maintenance**.

## The overall envelope: 14 years, peaks and troughs

Here is the annual distribution of City contracts over the period, in maximum notified amounts (sum of framework-agreement ceilings):

| Year | Contracts | Max envelope (€M) |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 2013 | 1,713 | 1,901 |
| 2014 | 1,483 | 1,530 |
| 2015 | 1,556 | 1,539 |
| 2016 | 1,601 | 1,917 |
| 2017 | 1,698 | **3,466** |
| 2018 | 1,458 | 2,836 |
| 2019 | 1,338 | 2,303 |
| 2020 | 1,196 | 3,282 |
| 2021 | 1,570 | **5,483** |
| 2022 | 1,281 | 3,825 |
| 2023 | 1,336 | 2,607 |
| 2024 | 1,762 | 3,220 |

The envelope does not follow a monotonic trajectory. The peaks — **2017** (€3.5Bn), **2021** (€5.5Bn) — correspond to years in which several **multi-year framework agreements** were notified at the same time. A year rich in notifications is not synonymous with a year rich in spending: a 5-year, €500M framework agreement can be notified in year N and executed gradually until N+5.

Cumulated over the 12 complete years (2013-2024), the aggregate exceeds **€35Bn in notified ceilings** — a considerable volume, set against the fact that no public-facing observatory keeps this ranking up to date year by year.

## Five activity families dominate

Grouping these 15 players by sector, **five families** emerge:

### 1. Cleaning and waste collection — 5 champions
[Polysotis](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/444578389), [Polyceja](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/533753216), [Sepur](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/350050589), [Urbapropreté IDF](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/813466174), [Polyreva](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/750043937): **five of the top fifteen City suppliers are urban-cleaning or waste-collection operators**. Cumulative: ~€1.47Bn over 14 years. This is the most fragmented sector in the ranking — Paris splits its cleaning contracts between several providers, by geographic or functional lots (collection vs. street cleaning, household waste vs. bulky items, etc.). No monopoly, but a stable local oligopoly.

### 2. Construction-roadworks — 3 champions
[Eurovia Île-de-France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/420948226) (€287M across 64 contracts), [Urbaine de Travaux](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/321057978) (€259M across 34 contracts), [Bouygues Bâtiment IDF](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/433900834) (€237M across 9 contracts), [Entreprise Fayolle](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/501639165) (€202M). **Maintenance and renovation of roadways, sidewalks and municipal facilities is carried out by 3-4 recurring players**, present almost every year over the period.

### 3. Urban furniture and signage — 1 historic champion
**[JCDecaux France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/622044501) at €314M across 17 contracts**, present throughout the 2013-2024 period. The overall contract for Paris urban furniture (bus shelters, information furniture, free-floating bicycles at one time) is one of the stable pillars of Paris public procurement.

### 4. Energy and public lighting — 1 massive newcomer
[CIELIS](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/900137175) at **€569M for a single contract in 2021**: this is presumably a multi-year framework agreement for public lighting (a sector where the City renewed its contracts during the period). And **[Octopus Energy France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/803248467) at €253M for 2 contracts in 2023** marks the entry of a new player in electricity supply — a symptom of the opening of the energy market to alternative providers.

### 5. IT and maintenance — 2 quiet champions
[SCC France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/424982650) (€222M across 31 contracts, present throughout the period) for IT equipment and services. [Maintenance Industrie](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/325807220) (€192M across 32 contracts) for technical maintenance of municipal buildings. Two players never in the media but structurally embedded.

## The "permanence" criterion: who has been there since the start

Of the top 15 suppliers, **8 have been active since 2013** — meaning they have had at least one City contract in every period, without interruption:

- [JCDecaux France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/622044501)
- [Eurovia IDF](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/420948226)
- [Urbaine de Travaux](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/321057978)
- [Polysotis](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/444578389) (until 2023)
- [Polyceja](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/533753216) (until 2022)
- [Sepur](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/350050589) (until 2023)
- [SCC France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/424982650)
- [Korrigan](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/441408812) (until 2023)

These eight operators constitute the **permanent core of Paris public procurement** over the period. They collectively receive **€2.2Bn** in City contracts across 14 years. Their identity — operators specialised in cleaning, construction-roadworks, urban furniture, IT and industrial maintenance, mostly French SMEs and mid-caps — is less present in public debate than that of consulting firms or signature event projects, even though it captures the most stable share of the envelope.

<aside className="fx-sidebar">

### Why so much annual variation

The annual notified-contracts envelope **is not the City's annual budget**. Three key differences:

1. **A 2021 notification for a 5-year framework agreement** spreads its spending until 2026
2. **The "maximum amount"** of a framework agreement is a ceiling — actual spend can be much lower (typically 50-80%)
3. **Annual peaks** often correspond to renewals of large contracts (cleaning every 5-7 years, public lighting, maintenance) that synchronize

Hence the importance of reasoning on **multi-year cumulatives** rather than on differences between isolated years.

</aside>

## New entrants: a market that is opening

Three notable facts in the recent period (2021-2024):

1. **[Octopus Energy France](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/803248467)** enters the ranking in 2023 — a symptom of energy-market deregulation (which allows the City to leave EDF/Engie)
2. **[Entreprise Fayolle](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/501639165)** emerges in 2021 (€202M cumulative) — increased competition in construction-roadworks, traditionally dominated by [Eurovia](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/420948226) and [Urbaine de Travaux](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/321057978)
3. **[CIELIS](/fr/city/paris/marches/fournisseur/900137175)** appears in 2021 with a mega-contract — presumably a new provider for public lighting

These entries suggest a **real fluidity in Paris public procurement**: it is not always the same players who dominate. But in parallel, the core of 8 historic players has remained stable since 2013.

## What these numbers do not say

This analysis stops at what the open-public-data pipeline allows us to measure. It does not say:

- **The final executed amount of each framework agreement** — the maximum notified envelope differs from the amount actually disbursed. Without access to final settlements, the ceiling/realized ratio cannot be calculated.
- **The possible presence of suppliers behind multi-contractor markets** — about €1.5Bn per year is notified under this label without an individualized contractor. Some of the "real champions" could be hidden there.
- **The contracts of attached operators** (CASVP, Paris Habitat, RIVP, Elogie-Siemp, Paris Musées) — which collectively consume several billion per year and are not in this strictly Ville de Paris scope.
- **Price changes** — the same supplier may have seen its unit prices vary over 14 years, which affects the raw cumulative-amount comparison.

What it does say, in a sentence: **the leading cumulative suppliers of Paris public procurement over 14 years are French SMEs and mid-cap firms specialised in cleaning, construction-roadworks, urban furniture, energy, IT and industrial maintenance**. These sectors capture the most stable share of the envelope and are less present in public debate than consultancies or major CAC 40 groups.
