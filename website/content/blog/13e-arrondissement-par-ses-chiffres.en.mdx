The 13th arrondissement is not, like the [19th](/analyses/19e-arrondissement-par-ses-chiffres), the arrondissement with the highest SRU rate. But it is, in absolute terms, **the one that hosts the most social-housing units in Paris**: **15,700 units identified** in the open-public-data pipeline, spread across **364 programs**. That's about **30% more than the 19th**, at a comparable SRU rate (34% vs 40%).

This particularity — a great deal of social housing, but at a moderate ratio because the arrondissement is also very populous — makes the 13th a case apart. It is also the arrondissement that experienced **two of the largest Paris urban-development operations of the second half of the 20th century**: **Les Olympiades** in the 1970s, and **Paris Rive Gauche** since 1991. Together, these two ZACs structure a major share of the contemporary built environment in southeastern Paris.

## The structure of the social-housing stock: Paris Habitat largely dominant

Of the 15,700 conventioned social-housing units in the 13th identified by the pipeline, **one social-housing operator largely dominates**:

| Social-housing operator | Units managed in the 13th | Share of 13th stock |
|---|---:|---:|
| Paris Habitat-OPH | 4,854 | 30.9% |
| RIVP (Régie Immobilière Ville de Paris) | 3,839 | 24.5% |
| Elogie-Siemp | 2,170 | 13.8% |
| **City triumvirate** | **10,863** | **69.2%** |
| Adoma | 816 | 5.2% |
| Sablière (ICF Habitat) | 640 | 4.1% |
| Coallia | 464 | 3.0% |
| Habitation Confortable | 384 | 2.4% |
| 1001 Vies Habitat | 352 | 2.2% |
| Efidis | 329 | 2.1% |
| Other operators | ~1,850 | ~11.8% |

Notable: **the City public-operator triumvirate (Paris Habitat + RIVP + Elogie-Siemp) captures 69% of the stock** — a concentration even stronger than in the 19th (54%). The 13th is, structurally, **the favored ground of Paris Habitat-OPH**, which alone manages nearly a third of the arrondissement's social-housing units.

## The pillar programs: all post-2010, all concentrated on the southern ring

The five largest programs in the 13th were delivered mostly between 2014 and 2018, and are mainly located on the **southern ring of the arrondissement** (Porte de Vitry, Porte de Choisy, Tolbiac), close to the city limits:

| Program | Operator | Year | Units |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| 15 avenue de la Porte de Vitry | Paris Habitat | 2018 | 833 |
| 28 avenue de la Porte de Choisy | Paris Habitat | 2018 | 457 |
| 183-185 rue du Chevaleret | Elogie-Siemp | 2016 | 433 |
| 18 rue Wurtz | Paris Habitat | 2018 | 405 |
| 10 rue Guyton de Morveau / 212 rue de Tolbiac | RIVP | 2016 | 386 |

This geographic concentration on the southern gates is the direct result of **Paris Rive Gauche**: the ZAC, which extends over nearly 130 hectares of railway brownfields released by the SNCF, has since 1991 enabled the gradual delivery of several thousand conventioned units (typically 30 to 50% of programs, depending on the brief set by the developer SemPariSeine).

## The two ZACs that changed everything

The contemporary 13th is, to a degree no other Paris arrondissement matches, **a product of large-scale planned urbanism**. Two operations in particular have reshaped the built environment and demographics:

### 1. Les Olympiades (1969-1977)
On the land freed up from the Tolbiac freight station, the "Italie 13" operation (later Les Olympiades) delivered **8 towers of 36 stories** and **a 1-km-long pedestrian deck**. Initial programming was oriented toward upscale free-market housing — which did not find its market — then gradually converted to intermediate and social housing. Part of the built fabric was acquired by public operators in the 1980s-1990s. Les Olympiades also became the epicenter of **Paris's Asian community** (the first French Chinatown) starting in the 1970s.

### 2. Paris Rive Gauche (1991-present)
The largest contemporary Paris urban-development operation, on **130 hectares** of SNCF brownfields between Avenue de France and the Seine. Mixed programming: 7,500 housing units (~40% conventioned), 1.1 million m² of office space, public facilities including the Bibliothèque François-Mitterrand (1995), Université Paris Cité, and the Halle Freyssinet (Station F). The ZAC is still being completed, with sectors such as **Bruneseau Nord** whose deliveries extend to 2030+.

These two operations alone account for a very significant share of the 15,700 social-housing units in the 13th — and clearly differentiate this arrondissement from the 19th (where social housing is more dispersed, inherited from smaller operations spread over a century).

## 32 projects delivered in 2024, €18.7M

For fiscal year 2024, the open-public-data pipeline identifies in the 13th **32 geolocated City investment projects**, totaling **€18.7M**. A volume comparable to that of the 19th (30 projects / €21.7M), which suggests a sustained maintenance and modernization policy in both arrondissements with large public housing stocks.

## The 13th compared to the 19th — two models of social-housing arrondissement

While the 19th and the 13th share their status as "high social-housing" arrondissements, they differ on three axes:

| Criterion | 19th | 13th |
|---|---|---|
| SRU rate (main stock) | ~40% | ~34% |
| Social-housing units (absolute) | 12,170 | 15,700 |
| Number of programs | 309 | 364 |
| Dominant operator | RIVP (25.6%) | Paris Habitat (30.9%) |
| City triumvirate concentration | 54.3% | 69.2% |
| Urbanization model | Diffuse, 1920-2000 legacy | Concentrated, two structuring ZACs |
| 2024 City investment (localized) | €21.7M | €18.7M |

The 19th is the arrondissement of **diversity** (many operators, many medium-sized programs, cumulative legacy). The 13th is the arrondissement of **planning** (few operators, larger programs, ZAC model). Two variants of the same phenomenon: the over-representation of social housing in the northeastern and southern parts of Paris.

<aside className="fx-sidebar">

### Why so much Paris Habitat in the 13th

Paris Habitat-OPH is the historic operator of the great planned-urbanism operations of 20th-century Paris. When the City (through SemPariSeine, the main developer of Paris Rive Gauche) contracted with an operator to carry out new programs delivered since 1995, Paris Habitat was very often selected — for two reasons:

1. **Its public status** (Office Public de l'Habitat) guarantees a strictly social and long-term orientation, with no minority private shareholding to arbitrate
2. **Its size** (the largest OPH in France, see our [Paris Habitat portrait](/analyses/paris-habitat-premier-bailleur-social)) allows it to absorb major programs without saturating its balance sheet

This concentration is not neutral. It creates an **operational dependency**: if Paris Habitat were to be restructured, the 13th would be more exposed than the average Paris arrondissement.

</aside>

## What these numbers do not say

This analysis stops at what the open-public-data pipeline allows us to measure. It does not say:

- **The population and social composition** of the 13th (INSEE publishes these data but they are not integrated into the pipeline at this date)
- **Vacancy, occupancy and waiting-list rates** in the social-housing stock — which are the responsibility of the operators (Paris Habitat, RIVP, Elogie-Siemp) and the DRIHL Île-de-France
- **The impact of the Asian community** on the economic life of the 13th — a topic documented in urban sociology but outside the scope of the financial pipeline
- **The completion of the final phases of Paris Rive Gauche** (Bruneseau Nord, neighboring ZAC Bercy-Charenton) — data to be supplemented as deliveries occur

What it does say, in a sentence: **the 13th arrondissement combines the largest Paris social-housing stock in absolute terms with an extreme concentration on Paris Habitat-OPH, a direct legacy of the two great ZACs that have shaped the arrondissement over 50 years**. It is a particularly instructive case for understanding how a long-term urban-development policy produces, more than any other mechanism, social housing that actually gets built.
