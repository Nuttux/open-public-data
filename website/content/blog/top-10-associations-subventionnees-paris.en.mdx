In fiscal year 2024, the Ville de Paris paid out **€1,353 million** in grants to **5,948 distinct beneficiaries**. The word "grant" spontaneously evokes neighbourhood associations, humanitarian NGOs, sports federations. The reality is more structured: of these €1.35Bn, **only €280M — i.e. 21%** — go to associations strictly speaking (1901 Act). The rest feeds a cascade of public operators, delegated companies and various legal structures that each, in their own way, deliver public-service missions.

This split is neither a malfunction nor a secret. It is the signature of a grant policy in which the City massively outsources its own action — social, cultural, urban — to entities legally tied to it. But it deserves to be named, measured, and looked at family by family.

## Where "grant" money goes

Breakdown of the €1,353M in 2024 by legal nature of the beneficiary:

| Family | Beneficiaries | Amount | Share |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Public establishments (EPL, EPCC, public-service entities, school funds…) | 201 | €816M | **60.3%** |
| Associations (1901 Act) | 2,769 | €280M | 20.7% |
| Companies (SEM, SPL, EPIC, delegated companies) | 726 | €146M | 10.8% |
| Other structures (mutuals, unions, foundations…) | 1,088 | €91M | 6.7% |
| State (prefecture, deconcentrated services) | 6 | €21M | 1.5% |
| Individuals (scholarships, individual aid) | 1,158 | €1M | 0.1% |
| **Total** | **5,948** | **€1,353M** | **100%** |

The all-categories number 1 is the **CASVP** (Centre d'Action Sociale de la Ville de Paris), at **€416.6M** — i.e. **31%** of all "grants" paid by the City that year. It is not an association: it is a public administrative establishment, legally separate but entirely steered by the City. The "grant" here is in fact an operating endowment between two linked entities.

## The cascade of public operators

The **201 public establishments** that capture **€816M** are not a homogeneous block. Four main families:

### 1. CASVP — the outsized social operator (€416.6M)
On its own, the Centre d'Action Sociale de la Ville de Paris receives **31%** of the entire grants envelope. It is a public administrative establishment, created in 1986, which manages municipal social aid (top-up RSA, allowances, hostels, Restaurants Émeraude, municipal nursing homes). The CASVP "grant" is in fact the **main budget endowment** of this entity — without it, the operator does not function. It is outsourcing by legal construction, not an annual choice.

### 2. City cultural operators (Paris Musées, Cité de la Musique…)
Paris Musées (€65.1M) manages the 14 free municipal museums. The Cité de la Musique (€15.9M) runs the Philharmonie. These are EPCCs (public cultural-cooperation establishments), legally separate but operationally integrated into City cultural policy.

### 3. Social-housing operators (Paris Habitat, Elogie-Siemp, RIVP)
Paris Habitat-OPH (€48.9M), RIVP (€44.2M), Elogie-Siemp (€24.8M). These endowments cover renovation operations, deficit-balanced new builds, or land carrying within the framework of pre-emption.

### 4. School funds, public-service entities, urban-development operators
Arrondissement school funds (€16.5M for the 18th's fund alone — school catering is run by 20 independent funds), Solidéo (€26.6M — Olympic operator in wind-down), Agence Métropolitaine des Mobilités Partagées (€23.5M), Régie de l'École Supérieure de Physique-Chimie Industrielles (€19.9M). All operators created to deliver a specific mission with management autonomy.

## Delegated companies (€146M)

The **726 "subsidised companies"** include mixed-economy companies (SEM), local public companies (SPL), and a limited number of private companies delegated a service. Number 1: **SNCF Réseau (€37.5M)**, a railway EPIC. The City "subsidises" SNCF Réseau for participation in station refurbishments, line extensions, footbridges. Here too, the word "grant" covers co-financing between public actors, not a donation to the private sector.

## The long tail — 5,947 beneficiaries below the top 10

Beyond the top 10, you enter a long-tail distribution. Breakdown for 2024 (all legal natures combined):

| Bracket | Number of beneficiaries | Cumulative |
|---|---:|---:|
| > €10M | 24 | €612M |
| €1M – €10M | 181 | €521M |
| €100k – €1M | 642 | €156M |
| €10k – €100k | 1,870 | €56M |
| &lt; €10k | 3,231 | €8M |

*Reading: 3,231 beneficiaries received less than €10,000 each in 2024, i.e. cumulative 0.6% of the total.*

This very wide base is carried by the **1,158 individuals** (artists, researchers, students on grants) who receive on average €940 each, and by the **1,088 "other structures"** (mutuals, unions, foundations, welfare committees).

<aside className="fx-sidebar">

### Method · what is a "grant" according to this file?

The Open Data Paris portal publishes a `subventions-associations.csv` file (annual edition) which aggregates **all City deliberations including a payment to a third-party entity**. The term "grant" in this file does not distinguish between:

- a grant strictly speaking (donation without market consideration)
- an endowment to a public establishment (intra-public transfer)
- a project co-financing (with agreement)
- a contribution to an urban-development operation (towards SEM/SPL)

This is what explains the gap between the spontaneous picture ("€1.35Bn to associations") and the accounting reality ("€1.35Bn paid to third-party entities, of which 60% are public operators").

</aside>

## How Paris compares

Among the major French metropolises, concentration on public operators is typical of all regional capitals. According to OFGL 2023 data, the "public operators / real associations" ratio ranges from 50% (Lyon) to 75% (Marseille). Paris at 60% is in the upper median.

The Parisian specificity is not the *share* of public operators — it is the **absolute number of operators**. With 201 EPL, EPCC, public-service entities and other satellites as beneficiaries, Paris is by far the French local authority that has outsourced the most distinct missions to legally separate entities.

## What these figures do not say

This analysis stops at what the annual deliberations allow us to measure. It does not say:
- Whether each grant is *well used* — that would require accessing the financial reports, which are not systematically published as open data
- Whether the City → public operator → final beneficiary cascade is *more efficient* than direct in-house execution
- Whether the number of operators (201) is *too high* — there is no normative threshold

What it does say, in one sentence: **6 out of 10 euros called "grants" never leave the public-service perimeter**; they move from one fund to another within a delegated execution structure. This is neither overreach nor a secret. It is the signature of a coherent, inherited and economically defensible outsourcing policy. But it deserves to be named for what it is: not spending towards the associative fabric, but an internal organisation of Parisian public service in a fragmented legal form.
